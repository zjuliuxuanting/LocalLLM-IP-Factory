# 海豚爆音：click burst pulse sound的声学机制与社交功能

> Source: https://dolphins.org/acoustics
**Acoustics** (Dolphin Research Center)

本文详细介绍了海豚声音产生的解剖学机制、click/whistle/burst-pulse三种声音类型的功能差异及其在社交互动中的作用。

**核心发现：**
Dolphins make many types of underwater sounds. The three that are most well-known are their whistles, clicks, and burst pulses. Clicks emitted by dolphins are thought to be exclusively used for echolocation, the dolphin's amazing ability to gather information about its world through sound.

**Click/Echolocation（回声定位click）：**
- **Production Mechanism**：Clicks are produced in rapid sequence, called "click trains," that sound to us like a creaking door or loud buzz. The clicks are produced so rapidly, you have to have special equipment to hear just one of them
- **Frequency Range**：The frequency range for echolocation clicks is typically between 40 kHz to 150 kHz, which is far beyond human hearing (20 Hz - 20 kHz)
- **Speed**：Can produce clicks at rates of up to 1,000 clicks per second
- **Directionality**：Highly directional, allowing dolphins to focus the sound beam for precise echolocation

**Whistle Communication（哨声沟通）：**
- **Signature Whistle**：One whistle which dolphins use most frequently, called a signature whistle, appears to serve as identification of individual dolphins since each dolphin develops a signature whistle that is uniquely its own. Dolphins do not appear to be born with a signature whistle. Calves develop their signature whistles over a four to six month period after birth
- **Frequency Range**：The approximate frequency range of bottlenose dolphin whistles is 0.2 to 24 kHz
- **Mimicry Ability**：Dolphins have an exceptional ability to mimic sounds and have been recorded making the signature whistles of other dolphins

**Burst-Pulse Sounds（爆音脉冲）：**
- **Definition**：Rapid click trains that convey excitement or aggression during close-range social interactions
- **Function**：Used primarily for social bonding and emotional expression rather than echolocation
- **Characteristics**：The rapid succession of clicks creates a "barking" or "squeaking" sound that is distinct from the focused beams used for navigation

本文从解剖学角度详细分析了海豚声音产生的生理机制，特别是phonic lips和melon的结构功能。

**核心发现：**
Dolphins do not possess vocal cords like humans and other terrestrial mammals. Sound generation takes place within the nasal passages, located just beneath the blowhole. The primary structures responsible are a pair of soft tissue folds known as the phonic lips, sometimes called "monkey lips."

**Sound Production Mechanism（声音产生机制）：**
- **Phonic Lips**：Sound is generated when the dolphin pushes air from its lungs, through the nasal passages, and past the phonic lips. The air pressure causes the lips to vibrate rapidly, generating sound pulses
- **Air Recycling**：Unlike breathing, this sound production does not require air to be expelled. Instead, the air is shuttled and recycled between specialized air sacs within the head. This air-recycling mechanism allows the dolphin to produce continuous streams of sound, such as click trains, without having to surface for air
- **Dual Sound Production**：Because a dolphin has two independent phonic lip complexes, it can produce two distinct sounds simultaneously, such as a whistle and a click

**Sound Projection（声音投射）：**
Once generated, the sound pulses are transmitted forward into the water through the melon, a large, fatty organ in the dolphin's forehead. The melon acts as an acoustic lens, focusing the sound waves into a highly directional beam. This focused beam projects sound efficiently through the water.

**Types of Sound and Their Behavioral Roles（声音类型与行为角色）：**
- **Whistles**：Used primarily for communication and social bonding, with frequencies ranging from 0.2 to 24 kHz. The most significant is the "signature whistle," a unique frequency-modulated sound developed early in life. This signature whistle functions like a personal name
- **Clicks**：High-frequency, short-duration pulses used exclusively for echolocation. These clicks are emitted in rapid sequences called "click trains" and can reach peak frequencies of up to 150 kHz
- **Burst-pulse sounds**：Rapid click trains, a third category used during close-range social interactions, often conveying excitement or aggression
