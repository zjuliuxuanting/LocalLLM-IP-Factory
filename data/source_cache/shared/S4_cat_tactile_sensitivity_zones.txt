**The Touch Map: Where Cats Actually Want to Be Petted** (2026)

本文从行为学研究角度详细分析了猫咪触觉敏感度的区域差异及其背后的神经生物学机制。

**核心发现：**
Cats have strong preferences about where they are touched, when, how, and by whom. Getting it wrong does not just result in a scratch. It erodes trust over time and shapes how willing your cat is to seek contact with you in the future.

**The Green Zone: Where Most Cats Enjoy Being Touched（安全区）：**
- **The base of the ears**：The area just behind and below where the ears meet the skull is one of the most reliably positive touch spots. Many cats lean into pressure here and show visible signs of relaxation. This area contains scent glands, and touch here often triggers a pleasurable response similar to scent-marking behaviour
- **The cheeks and behind the whisker pads**：Another scent gland location. Petting here tends to be well received, and many cats will actively push their face into your hand
- **Under the chin and along the jaw**：Most cats find this area genuinely pleasant. It connects to grooming behaviours between affiliated cats
- **The top of the head between the ears**：Generally well accepted. Approaching from the side rather than directly overhead produces a better response
- **The base of the tail on the back**：Many cats show a positive response to gentle contact just where the tail meets the spine

**The Amber Zone: Proceed With Caution（警戒区）：**
- **The back and sides**：This is also where the first signs of overstimulation appear. Skin rippling, tail flicking, and subtle postural shifts are early warning signals your cat is nearing their limit
- **The legs and paws**：Most cats are sensitive about having their legs and paws handled. Paw pads are highly sensitive, and holding a leg removes a cat's ability to move freely
- **The neck and throat**：Some cats enjoy gentle touch along the sides of the neck. The throat itself is a vulnerable area

**The Red Zone: Areas Most Cats Dislike（危险区）：**
- **The belly**：This is the area most likely to result in a bite, and also the most commonly misread. When a cat rolls over and exposes their belly, it is a trust display. It is not an invitation to touch. The belly is among the most sensitive areas on a cat's body, and touching it triggers a protective reflex regardless of how much they trust you
- **The underside of the tail base**：While the top of the tail base is often positive, the underside is a very different experience for most cats
- **The tail itself**：Most cats dislike having their tail touched or held. It is connected to the spine, contains numerous nerve endings, and is a key structure for balance

**Reading the Signals During Petting（阅读信号）：**
Signs your cat is enjoying the contact: leaning into your hand, slow blinking, kneading, a low relaxed purr, half-closed eyes, and loose relaxed body posture. Signs your cat is reaching their limit: tail beginning to flick or twitch, skin rippling along the back, ears rotating backwards, whiskers drawing forward, sudden stillness, or the head turning to look at your hand. Any of these means stop now.

**How to Tell If Your Cat Is Overstimulated During Petting** (CatFlix, 2026)

本文从神经生物学角度分析了猫咪过刺激反应的生理机制及识别方法。

**核心发现：**
Cat overstimulation happens when repetitive touch overwhelms the feline nervous system. You can prevent bites by stopping every three seconds to check for consent and watching for skin ripples or a lashing tail.

**Why does your cat bite you while you are petting them?（为什么猫会在被抚摸时咬人？）：**
Domestic cats, known scientifically as Felis catus, possess an incredibly sensitive nervous system. Their hair follicles and skin contain highly tuned receptors designed to detect subtle movements in their environment. When we pet them, we stimulate these nerves. For many cats, this feels wonderful for a short time. However, the pleasure can turn into physical pain or intense irritation very quickly. This transition is the sensory processing threshold.

**The CatFlix Three-Step Consent Loop（三步同意循环）：**
1. **The Initial Knuckle Test**：I always offer my knuckle or a single finger before I touch my cat. I place my hand about three inches from his nose. I wait for him to close the gap
2. **The Three-Second Rule**：I limit my petting to three seconds at a time. After three strokes, I pull my hand back completely. I observe his reaction
3. **The Body Scan**：I constantly scan his body for micro signals while I pet him
